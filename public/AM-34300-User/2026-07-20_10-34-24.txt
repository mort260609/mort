  Poster Memorabilia — Design Brief
  Prepared for: VibeStyle Agency From: Sam Barry, Poster Memorabilia Date: July 2026
  
  
  1. The short version
  We sell hand-signed movie and music posters. We are repositioning from an e-commerce store into something closer to a gallery — and we need the site to carry that.
  
  The current site works. It does not feel like the business we are becoming.
  
  
  2. Who we are
  Poster Memorabilia has traded since 2019, built on more than two decades of experience in signed memorabilia. Every poster we sell is hand-signed by the talent themselves. The poster is a high-quality reproduction at collector size (27×40" film, 24×36" music) — the signature is the piece, and that is what we stake our name on.
  
  Every piece is authenticated in-house, issued a serial-numbered Certificate of Authenticity and a tamper-evident hologram, and registered permanently at AutographRegistry.com, our own registration system.
  
  Two guarantees underpin everything:
  
  Authenticity, for life. If a signature we sold is ever proven fake, every cent back. No time limit.
  90-day money-back. Any piece, any reason, full refund.
  
  
  3. Who we sell to
  Our collectors are fans first. They buy the film or band that mattered to them — the movie they watched with their father, the album that carried them through a year. Purchases are emotional, often personal, frequently gifts.
  
  Most are adults buying for their own walls. Some are seasoned collectors building deliberately; many are first-timers spending several hundred dollars on a signature they cannot verify themselves — and quietly nervous about it.
  
  That nervousness is the design problem. Every question they ask — condition, authenticity, shipping, framing — is really the same question: can I trust you? The site's job is to answer it before it is asked, through restraint and evidence rather than reassurance graphics.
  
  They are not bargain hunters. They wait for the right piece. Urgency tactics and discount messaging actively lose them.
  
  
  4. Where we're going
  From store to gallery. Not a shop that sells posters — a house that places pieces with collectors.
  
  The written voice is already built and live: measured warmth, no hype, no scarcity language, expertise given freely, honesty as the proof of quality. We say collector, not customer. Piece, not item. The design needs to sit in that same register.
  
  We are also building a concierge layer — named client-facing people who advise on framing, hold pieces, and offer collectors first sight of new arrivals before public listing. The site should have room for that to exist visibly.
  
  
  5. What we want the site to feel like
  Restrained. Confidence expressed through space and omission. No starbursts, countdown timers, urgency banners, or discount badges — permanently, not just for now.
  Considered. Fewer, larger images. The pieces are 27×40" artworks; the site should treat them that way.
  Quietly authoritative. The authentication story is our strongest asset. It should be present and legible without shouting.
  Personal. A real house with real people, not a storefront.
  5a. Reference points
  House of Spoils ( www.houseofspoils.com )— the closest register to what we want. This is the primary reference. What we take from it: the confidence to leave space, the refusal to shout, imagery treated as the main subject rather than as product shots, and a tone that assumes the visitor has taste rather than explaining things to them. If you understand why House of Spoils works, you understand this brief.
  
  Louis Vuitton — the maison model. Not the monogram or the price point, but the structure underneath: a house with a history, a craft story told without apology, and the sense that buying is entry into something rather than a transaction. Note how the product is never discounted, never urgent, and always presented as the outcome of expertise. Our authentication process is our equivalent of their atelier — it should be presented with the same quiet pride.
  
  Auction houses and fine-art galleries — Phillips, Sotheby's editions, contemporary print galleries. For provenance presentation, single-work layouts, and how documentation is shown as part of the object's story rather than as a trust badge.
  
  Anti-references: mass-market memorabilia retailers, sports card and trading card sites, anything with "SALE" in a red circle, any layout built around urgency or scarcity messaging.
  
  
  6. Scope
  Platform: Shopify. [CONFIRM: theme rebuild vs custom theme vs new theme purchase + customisation]
  
  Key pages / templates:
  
  Homepage
  Collection / category pages
  Product page — the most important template. Must handle: large imagery, signature close-ups, condition, COA and hologram, registry reference, guarantees, framing guidance
  Authenticity page (copy already written and live — needs design)
  About / philosophy
  Concierge / collector services [NEW — to be designed]
  FAQ, shipping, returns, contact
  Cart and checkout styling within Shopify's limits
  
  Also needed:
  
  Email template design direction, so our email marketing aligns [our email team is waiting on this]
  Basic brand guidelines: type, palette, spacing, image treatment, button and form styling
  Mobile is the priority — [CONFIRM % of traffic] of our traffic is mobile
  
  Out of scope for now: logo redesign [CONFIRM], photography, copywriting (we write our own).
  
  
  7. Constraints and non-negotiables
  Shopify platform, existing catalogue structure
  Product photography is what it is for now — design should flatter it
  Condition labels display "used" for platform reasons on signed pieces; the design should not make that jarring
  Must accommodate the authentication and guarantee messaging without turning it into trust-badge clutter
  Site must not read as discount-driven at any point
  
  
  8. Practicalities
  Budget: 3-5K USD
  Timeline: 1-3 MONTHS
  Decision maker: Sam Barry, direct
  Development: [CONFIRM — do you build, or design-only with our developer implementing?]

  Your read on the positioning — does the gallery direction hold, and where would you push it further?
  Proposed scope and phasing
  Timeline and cost
  Two or three relevant examples of work in an adjacent register
  
  
  10. One line to design against
  A gallerist who is confident, unhurried, and slightly more interested in the piece than in the sale.
  
  If a design decision doesn't fit that sentence, it's the wrong decision.
