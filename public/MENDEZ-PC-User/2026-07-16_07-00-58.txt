  THE SYSTEM: Core Design Philosophy
  1. The Emotional Journey of the User
  The emotional journey is not about convenience or organization—it is about ascension. When the user interfaces with THE SYSTEM, the underlying emotion should be a sense of profound gravity. They are not opening a tool; they are stepping into an environment built specifically for their evolution.
  
  Initial Contact: Curiosity mixed with the weight of responsibility. An understanding that this space demands focus.
  Engagement: Clarity and alignment. The noise of daily life is stripped away, leaving only the signal of what truly matters.
  Fulfillment: Not the cheap dopamine of a checked checkbox, but the quiet satisfaction of a system acknowledging their progress. A feeling of being monitored, guided, and pushed forward by an entity that demands their best.
  2. The Transformation Stages
  The user is not "creating an account" or "using features." They are undergoing a continuous recruitment and transformation process.
  
  Activation (The Outsider): The initial engagement where the user commits to the process. The system awakens to their presence.
  Identity (The Participant): The system establishes who the user is within its parameters. What are their baselines? What is their current state?
  Objective Definition (The Architect): The user defines their trajectory. This is not goal-setting; it is programming the coordinates of their evolution.
  Status Creation (The Operator): The system begins generating real-time telemetry on the user's progress. The user becomes an operator of their own life.
  Progression (The Evolved): Continuous cycles of directives and acknowledgements, refining the user's state over time.
  3. The Narrative Structure
  THE SYSTEM operates on a strict narrative: "You are capable of more, and I am the infrastructure that will guarantee it." The interface speaks with quiet authority. It does not beg for engagement with notifications or colorful badges. It waits, holding the user's trajectory, and responds only to meaningful input. The narrative shifts from "What do I need to do today?" to "What is the system directing me to execute to advance my state?"
  
  4. The Philosophy Behind Each Screen
  Every screen is a state of transformation.
  
  Activation (formerly Login): A gateway. The moment the user crosses the threshold from the chaotic outside world into the structured environment of THE SYSTEM.
  Command Center (formerly Dashboard): The focal point of reality. It answers immediately: Who are you? Where are you? What is the current directive? It is devoid of clutter, presenting only the absolute truth of the user's trajectory.
  Status Window (formerly Profile): A living telemetry readout of the user's evolution. It reflects their current state, their accumulated progress, and their standing relative to their objectives. It is a mirror, not a settings page.
  Directives (formerly Tasks): Issued commands from the system, aligned with the user's objectives. They carry weight. Completing them is an act of progression, acknowledged formally by the system.
  Access (formerly Settings): The underlying configuration of the system's parameters. Modifying these alters the rules of engagement.
  5. How THE SYSTEM Differs from Productivity Apps
  Productivity apps are passive tools; THE SYSTEM is an active partner.
  
  Focus: Productivity apps focus on doing. THE SYSTEM focuses on becoming.
  Emotion: Productivity apps aim for relief (getting things out of your head). THE SYSTEM aims for tension (holding you to a standard).
  Interface: Productivity apps are loud, colorful, and gamified to keep you clicking. THE SYSTEM is dark, confident, minimal, and silent. It does not want your clicks; it wants your execution.
  Completion: A productivity app gives you a little animation when you check a box. THE SYSTEM acknowledges the completion of a directive as a shift in your fundamental status.

  THE SYSTEM: Core Design Philosophy
  1. The Emotional Journey of the User
  The emotional journey is not about convenience or organization—it is about ascension. When the user interfaces with THE SYSTEM, the underlying emotion should be a sense of profound gravity. They are not opening a tool; they are stepping into an environment built specifically for their evolution.
  
  Initial Contact: Curiosity mixed with the weight of responsibility. An understanding that this space demands focus.
  Engagement: Clarity and alignment. The noise of daily life is stripped away, leaving only the signal of what truly matters.
  Fulfillment: Not the cheap dopamine of a checked checkbox, but the quiet satisfaction of a system registering their progress. A feeling of operating within a precise infrastructure that demands their absolute best.
  2. The Transformation Stages
  The user is not "creating an account" or "using features." They are undergoing a continuous recruitment and transformation process.
  
  Activation (The Outsider): The initial engagement where the user commits to the process. The system awakens to their presence.
  Identity (The Participant): The system establishes who the user is within its parameters. What are their baselines? What is their current state?
  Objective Definition (The Architect): The user defines their trajectory. This is not goal-setting; it is programming the coordinates of their evolution.
  Status Creation (The Operator): The system begins generating real-time telemetry on the user's progress. The user becomes an operator of their own life.
  Progression (The Evolved): Continuous cycles of directives and acknowledgements, refining the user's state over time.
  3. The Narrative Structure
  THE SYSTEM operates strictly as infrastructure, not as a character or AI narrator. It does not speak with motivational speeches or engage in roleplay. Its power derives purely from absolute clarity. The core narrative is simply the transmission of truth: "THE SYSTEM knows where I am, where I'm going, and what matters right now."
  
  The interface is calm, precise, and confident. It uses minimal words. It does not beg for engagement with notifications. The relationship is strictly Operator ↔ System, completely devoid of "User ↔ App" or "Player ↔ Character" dynamics. The device is merely a terminal accessing the living state of the Operator's evolution.
  
  4. The Philosophy Behind Each Screen
  Every screen is a state of transformation.
  
  Activation (formerly Login): A gateway. The moment the user crosses the threshold from the chaotic outside world into the structured environment of THE SYSTEM.
  Command Center (formerly Dashboard): The focal point of reality. It answers immediately: Who are you? Where are you? What is the current directive? It is devoid of clutter, presenting only the absolute truth of the user's trajectory.
  Status Window (formerly Profile): A living telemetry readout of the user's evolution. It reflects their current state, their accumulated progress, and their standing relative to their objectives. It is a mirror, not a settings page.
  Directives (formerly Tasks): Issued commands from the system, aligned with the user's objectives. They carry weight. Completing them is an act of progression, acknowledged formally by the system.
  Access (formerly Settings): The underlying configuration of the system's parameters. Modifying these alters the rules of engagement.
  5. How THE SYSTEM Differs from Productivity Apps
  Productivity apps are passive tools designed for convenience; THE SYSTEM is infrastructure designed for ascension.
  
  Focus: Productivity apps focus on doing. THE SYSTEM focuses on becoming.
  Emotion: Productivity apps aim for relief (getting things out of your head). THE SYSTEM aims for tension (holding you to a standard).
  Interface: Productivity apps are loud, colorful, and gamified to keep you clicking. THE SYSTEM is dark, confident, minimal, and silent. It does not want your clicks; it wants your execution.
  Completion: A productivity app gives you a little animation when you check a box. THE SYSTEM acknowledges the completion of a directive as a shift in your fundamental status.

  ## Part 2: Build the Zapper Contract
  
  Once the pool exists and has liquidity:
  
  - New contract, e.g. `NexaiZapper.sol` — takes a single token deposit
    (e.g. USDC), swaps the appropriate portion into WETH via
    `SwapRouter02` against the pool just seeded, then calls
    `NexaiCore.deposit()` with the resulting exact basket token amounts

  Here's what's built so far, and a walkthrough of the reasoning behind it.
  
  Part 1 — SeedPool.s.sol
  This is a Foundry script (mints test tokens, then creates + funds a Uniswap V3 pool):
  
  Pool creation & pricing. Uniswap V3 pools always store price as "token1 per token0," where token0/token1 are just whichever of the two addresses sorts numerically lower — not "whichever token we call WETH." So the script sorts the two addresses first, then figures out which reserve amount goes with which slot, rather than assuming WETH is always token0.
  The sqrtPriceX96 math. A V3 pool needs to be initialized with a starting price, encoded as sqrt(price) × 2^96. I computed this using OpenZeppelin's Math.mulDiv (does the multiply-then-divide in one wide 512-bit step) followed by Math.sqrt, instead of the naive sqrt(a/b) * 2^96, because multiplying by 2^192 directly before dividing would overflow a normal uint256 for token amounts in the range we're using. mulDiv sidesteps that.
  Full-range liquidity. Rather than concentrating liquidity in a narrow price band (the usual V3 selling point), I seeded across the entire allowed tick range for the 0.3% fee tier. Simpler, and appropriate for a test pool whose only job is "let the zapper's swap execute" rather than capital efficiency.
  Seeds 20 WETH / 40,000 USDC — 40× the 0.5:1000 basket ratio, as you specified.
  Part 2 — NexaiZapper.sol
  Takes USDC only, mints NEXAI, in one transaction:
  
  Reads the current basket weights from Core and replicates Core's own round-up formula to compute exactly how much WETH and USDC deposit() will demand. (This has to be duplicated rather than reused, since we're not changing NexaiCore.sol — a comment flags this so the two stay in sync if weights ever change.)
  Pulls a caller-specified maxUsdcIn from the user upfront — this is the slippage guard: it's the most USDC the user is willing to spend across both the direct USDC leg and the swap leg combined.
  Swaps just enough USDC → WETH using exactOutputSingle (not exactInputSingle) — because we know the exact WETH amount needed, asking Uniswap for "give me exactly this much WETH, spend at most this much USDC" is the more natural fit than guessing an input and refunding leftover WETH.
  Approves Core for the exact WETH/USDC amounts and calls deposit().
  The subtle bit: NexaiCore.deposit() mints NEXAI to msg.sender — but when the Zapper calls deposit(), the Zapper is msg.sender from Core's point of view, not the original user. Without an extra step, the newly minted NEXAI would get stuck in the Zapper. So the Zapper explicitly forwards the NEXAI it just received on to the real caller afterward.
  Refunds any unspent USDC, resets all approvals to zero, and ends the transaction holding nothing — no custody persists between transactions.
  Tests — NexaiZapper.t.sol
  I used a MockSwapRouter with a fixed exchange rate rather than a real Uniswap pool, so these tests check the Zapper's own accounting logic (approvals, refunds, forwarding) deterministically, without depending on live testnet state. Covers: correct end-to-end deposit + refund + zero-dust invariant, reverting when the slippage budget is too tight, reverting when the swap itself needs more than allowed, and reverting if the basket is ever reconfigured away from WETH/USDC.
  
  One honest gap: this sandbox doesn't have forge installed, so I traced the logic by hand rather than actually compiling/running the suite. Please run forge build && forge test --match-contract NexaiZapperTest -vvv locally before trusting this — I'd treat it as unverified until that's green.
